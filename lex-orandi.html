Lex orandi, lex credendi

Lex orandi, lex credendi. The law of prayer is the law of belief. This page is the praying side of the Credo.

The axiom comes from the fifth century, from Prosper of Aquitaine, a disciple of St. Augustine. The way a church prays shapes what it believes, and what it believes can be read off the way it prays. Doctrine is not a theory bolted onto worship. It rises out of the altar, the Baptism, and the daily office, which is why the undivided Church settled her creed and her liturgies together. Some extend the axiom with a third law, lex vivendi, the law of living, for what is prayed and believed must finally be lived. This page is about that side of the confession, the praying and the living, and it answers three questions in order. Where do I worship. How do I pray at home. How do I learn the faith.

Where one may worship: preferred

We hold that the deposit of faith is carried today in more than one communion, and this page will not decide your parish for you. We also hold, plainly, that heaven is reachable from any of the churches named below, for the Church is entered by faith and baptism, and every church here baptizes. What the book grades carefully, in its section 6, is a different question, which altars carry the full orders and sacraments of the undivided Church. Grades are about sacraments, not about who God can save.

One asymmetry explains two generations of loss. When liberalism came to the Reformation churches, they could prune. The confessional bodies split from the mainlines and stood, so the Protestant whose pulpit fell had a refuge inside his own tradition and a road to it. When the same liberalism came to the Catholic parishes it came inside a single structure, with no parallel body to receive the sheep, and the Catholic whose parish failed him too often concluded that the faith itself had failed him, and left, not for another altar but for none. For the sheep of a failing parish, what follows is a map of refuges, and knowing it is the difference between a believer who drives further and a believer who stops believing.

The ancient communions. The Roman Catholic Church, in her reverent parishes, the Latin Mass parishes, the Priestly Fraternity of St. Peter, the Ordinariate of the Chair of St. Peter, the Anglican patrimony inside Rome, and the Eastern Catholic churches. The Eastern Orthodox churches, findable through the Assembly of Bishops. The Oriental Orthodox churches, Coptic, Armenian, Syriac, Ethiopian, and Malankara. The Assyrian Church of the East. These carry the undivided Church’s orders, sacraments, and faith, and their division is a rupture within that inheritance, not a partition into strangers.

The Union of Scranton. The Polish National Catholic Church and the Nordic Catholic Church. Their Declaration of 2008 reads almost clause for clause as our confession does, and one may worship there gladly. The counsel forks by who is asking. For us, formed by staying, joining them would forfeit the standing our appeal requires. For the reader coming from elsewhere, or from a parish that failed him, they are not a wrong home, but the nearest thing to this confession with a roof on it. Same creed, different door.

The high and orthodox Anglicans. The Continuing Anglican churches and the high parishes of the Anglican Church in North America. They keep the creed, the sacraments, and a reverence in worship that has drawn many home to the ancient faith. Their orders carry an old question that only the party who raised it can settle, and their patrimony is honored enough that Rome has made a home for it in the Ordinariate. The provinces divide over the very moral consensus the book stakes, so the standing question for the Anglican is whether his province still holds what he holds, and the counsel is to hold the confession inside one that does.

The confessional Lutherans. The Lutheran Church Missouri Synod and the high-church confessional parishes. They confess the real presence and baptismal regeneration without qualification, and hold much of the undivided faith. What they lack is the historic episcopate, their pastors being ordained by other pastors, and whether that preserves the ministry is a real question the authors work out in The bishop and the presbyter, not one the book closes against them.

The conservative Reformed. The Presbyterian Church in America and the Orthodox Presbyterian Church. They hold the Scriptures, the ancient creeds, and the moral law with conviction, and they have kept them faithfully where others let them go. On baptism and the Supper we confess more than the Reformed standards do, and we say so plainly. On the ordained ministry and the succession the authors of the book are themselves divided, a question worked out in The bishop and the presbyter. Either way they are brethren in the deepest sense, and their witness belongs to the whole Body.

The counsel that goes with the map is short. Be rooted, then visit. Keep one home altar, a parish where you are known, confessed, and formed, and visit the other communions in charity. Where the visited altar is closed, as the Orthodox and most Oriental altars are, visiting is worship and not communing, and the closed altar is borne as part of the division’s cost, not resented.

The home altar binds you to a communion and its sacraments, not to the nearest building. Parish choice is free, and where a pulpit unsays the creed or the moral law over your children, the duty is to drive past it, to the reverent parish, the sound confessor, at whatever distance the case demands. This is not disloyalty. The precedent is the fourth century, when the faithful under Arian bishops sought orthodox altars across whatever boundaries stood between, and preserved the faith much of the hierarchy was losing.

One more distinction helps the hard cases. Preaching and sacraments are two different goods, and they do not have to come from the same building. If the nearest pulpit of your own communion preaches another gospel, and a faithful Lutheran or Presbyterian down the road preaches Christ crucified, your family’s ears are better off with the faithful sermon. But your confession and your communion do not move with your ears. They belong to your own altar, sought as far away as it must be sought.

Where one may worship: nuances

We hold that there is no salvation outside the Church, and we hold that the Church is entered by faith and baptism in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit. In necessity anyone can baptize, and the Church has always said so. If there were no Christian within a thousand miles and you asked a Muslim to pour the water and say the words, the baptism would be real, and heaven would recognize it. God is not bound by His own sacraments. The same mercy reaches further. The one who never had a fair chance to hear is not damned for geography, which the Church calls invincible ignorance, and we are permitted the hope, held as hope and not as certainty, that the mercy of God reaches all. In all of it Christ is the one Savior. His grace flows through the Church, and beyond her visible borders, and it is His, not ours. The Credo states this at full length.

Nor do we demand perfection of doctrine from pastors, or from your own past. If you met God in the Jesus movement, in an American revival tent, in a Baptist pew, the meeting was real, and nobody here wants to take it from you. We may argue with the theology of the tent. We do not argue with what God did in it. The position of the book is that the power and grace of God extend beyond the divisions and the splits, while doctrine and dogma still matter.

So where is the concern? It begins when a Christian moves away from the communions named above toward the fringe, the odd sect, the schism of a schism. That road is not putting unity first, and it opens a person to being formed by something less than the whole. A fellowship may truly have the gospel and still not have all that the apostles handed down, the Baptism of the children, the Supper that is more than a memorial, the ministry ordered in succession. Why ask the Muslim to baptize you when an Orthodox priest stands down the street, ordained in valid orders, in succession from the apostles, believing what St. Augustine believed? Both baptisms are real. Only one hands you the whole inheritance with the water. Why settle for less when God wants you to have it all in fullness.

None of this is a command to abandon anyone. Go to the Baptist church with Grandma, and gladly. But maybe go with her on Wednesday night, and reserve Sunday for a communion that is authentically first-millennium Christianity. Love what is real wherever you find it, and give your Sundays to the fullness.

To dig deeper before choosing, the charting papers grade every church named here from its own standards, with what catholicity asks of each by name: part one, the historic communions, part two, the free churches, and part three, the fifty objections.

A rule of prayer at home

The rule at home follows from the altar you have just chosen. The confession implies a pattern of devotion that is first-millennial, shared between East and West, and ordinary. Nothing here is exotic. It is what a serious layman of the undivided Church would recognize as his own week.

The Eucharist, weekly and more. The Mass or Divine Liturgy is the center of the week, and the rest of the rule follows from it.

The Hours. The Divine Office in any of its forms, the Monastic Diurnal, the older Breviary, or the Liturgy of the Hours. The daily Psalter is the first-millennium layman’s foundation.

The Jesus Prayer. Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me, a sinner. The shared contemplative tradition of East and West, taken up with the Philokalia and The Way of a Pilgrim.

Lectio divina. Scripture read slowly and prayerfully, with the Fathers as guides.

Confession and fasting. Regular penance, and the Ember days, Lent, and the ancient fasts.

The Mother of God. The Rosary in the West and the Akathist in the East, Marian devotion in the key of the Councils, that is, the Theotokos.

A rule and a rhythm. The Rule of St. Benedict and the life of an oblate, stability, the Office, and lectio, pre-schism and revered East and West, well suited to a layman seeking a first-millennium pattern of life.

A catechism of the first millennium

The reader who wants the faith of the undivided Church taught whole, in one voice and from inside, should have a catechism of that Church rather than a modern digest, and one exists. The Catechetical Lectures of St. Cyril of Jerusalem, delivered about the year 350 to the candidates for baptism in the church of the Holy Sepulchre, teach the creed article by article and then, in the mystagogical lectures, the sacraments the newly baptized had just received, baptism, chrismation, and the eucharist. They are pre-schism, honored in East and West alike, addressed to laymen, and complete. St. Gregory of Nyssa’s Catechetical Oration, printed in English as the Great Catechism, serves the teacher as St. Cyril serves the catechumen, and St. Augustine’s Enchiridion, on faith, hope, and love, is the Western pocket manual of the same age. All three stand in the standard nineteenth-century translations, Cyril in the Nicene and Post-Nicene Fathers, second series, volume 7, Gregory in the same series, volume 5, and the Enchiridion in the first series, volume 3.
