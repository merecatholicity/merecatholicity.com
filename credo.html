Credo

The confession of Mere Catholicity, and the sources that support it.
Call us first-millennium catholics, paleo-orthodox, Vincentians, or conservative ecumenists. The position is ecumenical because the Church is bigger than any one communion, and conservative because we receive and preserve the faith as it was handed down to us, and do not innovate upon it.

The confession

I hold the whole faith of the undivided Catholic Church: the Holy Scriptures, the Nicene Creed, the seven Ecumenical Councils, the apostolic succession and the government of bishops, the sacramental mysteries and the real presence of Christ in the Eucharist, the regeneration given in Baptism, and the consensus of the Fathers and the sensus fidelium of the first thousand years.

I honor the Bishop of Rome as first among equals, within the limits the Fathers of East and West both knew. I hold that Christ preserves His whole Church from final failure, and that outside this Church there is no salvation, because the Church is His Body, one across her present divisions, and it is entered by faith and baptism.

The Scriptures

We hold the Holy Scriptures as the supreme witness of the faith, in the full canon the ancient communions received, wider than the Protestant Old Testament. So the edition matters, because the communions do not carry the same books. You want a Bible whose canon and whose scholarship Roman, Orthodox, and Protestant readers can all trust. This is the one we recommend.

The New Oxford Annotated Bible with the Apocrypha, Revised Standard Version, Expanded Edition, Oxford University Press, 1982, genuine leather, black. The notes are edited by Bruce Metzger, a Presbyterian minister and one of the finest New Testament Greek scholars of his age, and the expanded Apocrypha carries the books the ancient communions count as Scripture, so Roman, Orthodox, and Protestant readers can open the same volume. The RSV itself is the last of the great common translations before gender-neutral revision, and it keeps thee and thou in address to God. A solid conservative and ecumenical pick.

The Thirty-Ninth Festal Letter, by St. Athanasius, written for Easter of 367. The first list of the books of Scripture as the Church received them, from the bishop who defended the Nicene faith through five exiles. Short, and worth reading whole, because the canon itself is a work of reception.

The Nicene Creed

We hold the Nicene Creed as the one definition of faith the whole Church received. Confessed at Nicaea in 325, completed at Constantinople in 381, it is recited to this day in every communion the confession addresses, which is why it stands here in full. If you can say this creed and mean it, you already hold the core this site is about.

I believe in one God,
the Father almighty,
maker of heaven and earth,
of all things visible and invisible.

I believe in one Lord Jesus Christ,
the Only Begotten Son of God,
born of the Father before all ages.
God from God, Light from Light,
true God from true God,
begotten, not made, consubstantial with the Father;
through him all things were made.
For us men and for our salvation
he came down from heaven,
and by the Holy Spirit was incarnate of the Virgin Mary,
and became man.
For our sake he was crucified under Pontius Pilate,
he suffered death and was buried,
and rose again on the third day
in accordance with the Scriptures.
He ascended into heaven
and is seated at the right hand of the Father.
He will come again in glory
to judge the living and the dead
and his kingdom will have no end.

I believe in the Holy Spirit, the Lord, the giver of life,
who proceeds from the Father and the Son,*
who with the Father and the Son is adored and glorified,
who has spoken through the prophets.

I believe in one, holy, catholic and apostolic Church.
I confess one Baptism for the forgiveness of sins
and I look forward to the resurrection of the dead
and the life of the world to come.

Amen.

Text as recited in the Roman rite, from the USCCB.
* The words and the Son, the filioque, are a Western addition. The East recites the Nicene Creed without them.

The seven Ecumenical Councils

We hold the seven councils of the undivided Church, from Nicaea in 325 to the second council of Nicaea in 787, as councils of the whole Body, defining the faith all the ancient communions still share. Councils held by one side alone after the divisions do not carry that authority for the whole.

The Seven Ecumenical Councils of the Undivided Church, edited by Henry R. Percival. The canons and dogmatic decrees of the seven councils, with the local synods that received ecumenical acceptance, the ancient epitomes, and Percival’s notes, in the standard English edition, Nicene and Post-Nicene Fathers, second series, volume 14. The confession holds what these councils defined, and the book’s whole argument is about how the Body came to receive them, so judge from the acts themselves rather than from summaries. Percival’s edition is big, but it is made for looking things up, not for reading straight through.

The apostolic succession and the government of bishops

We hold that bishops in succession from the apostles are the received government of the Church. This order was there from the beginning and everywhere, not invented by a later age, and the two witnesses below are as early as witnesses come.

The First Epistle of Clement to the Corinthians, written about the year 96, while the apostle John may still have been alive. Rome writes to Corinth that the apostles appointed bishops and provided for their succession, and that deposing worthy men breaks an order the apostles set up. The earliest statement of the principle there is.

The Epistles of St. Ignatius of Antioch. Seven short letters written on the road to martyrdom, about the year 107. If you read only one thing on this page, read these. What they show is not argued but assumed: one bishop with his presbyters and deacons in every city Ignatius writes to, within living memory of the apostles. If the government of bishops were a later corruption, these letters could not exist.
Read online (HTML) · Download (PDF)

Against Heresies, Book III, by St. Irenaeus of Lyons, about the year 180. Irenaeus answers the heretics’ claim to a secret tradition by pointing to the public succession of bishops in every church, a list anyone could check. That argument is the confession’s own logic, that the faith is what the churches received and handed on in the open, and the paper’s criterion of reception descends from it.

The sacramental mysteries

We hold the sacraments to be real means of grace, the mysteries the undivided Church celebrated from the start, not bare symbols or afterthoughts.

The Mystagogic Lectures, by St. Cyril of Jerusalem. The five lectures on the sacraments the newly baptized had just received, baptism, chrismation, and the eucharist. Here the undivided Church explains her own rites to her own newly baptized, from inside, with no later controversy in view. What she says they are, she says to catechumens, not to opponents.

The Didache, the Teaching of the Twelve Apostles, from about the end of the first century. The oldest church manual, with its instructions for baptism and the eucharist. A page of it says more about how the first Christians worshipped than a shelf of arguments.

The real presence of Christ in the Eucharist

We hold that the bread and wine of the Eucharist truly become the Body and Blood of Christ. The confession binds the fact, as the early Church taught it to her own children, and stays out of the later quarrels about explaining how.

On the Body and Blood of Christ, by St. Cyril of Jerusalem, the fourth Mystagogic Lecture. A plain statement of the change, made to catechumens about the year 350, seven centuries before the disputes about how. The confession holds what Cyril taught here, and no more than that.
Read online (HTML) · Download (PDF)

The First Apology, by St. Justin Martyr, written about the year 155 to the emperor himself. Chapter 61 describes baptism as regeneration and chapters 65 to 67 describe the Sunday eucharist, received not as common bread and not as common drink. The earliest description of Christian worship we have, addressed to outsiders with nothing to hide.

The regeneration given in Baptism

We hold that baptism truly does something. It regenerates, it forgives, and it joins a person to the Body of Christ. It is the door of the Church, not a mere sign of something that happened elsewhere.

On Baptism, by St. Cyril of Jerusalem, the third Catechetical Lecture. Regeneration taught to the candidates themselves, as the plain meaning of the font.

On Baptism, by Tertullian, about the year 200. The oldest surviving treatise on the sacrament, written against a sect that despised water baptism. Worth reading because it shows the doctrine already old enough to need defending, a century before Constantine.

The consensus of the Fathers

We hold that where the Fathers of the first thousand years agree, their agreement is a trustworthy witness to the faith the apostles left. No single Father is the measure. The consensus is.

The Commonitory, by Vincent of Lérins. The rule of the consensus itself, what has been believed everywhere, always, and by all, written in 434 as a working method for telling the faith from novelty. The whole paper is one long application of this rule, so keep the source in hand.

The sensus fidelium

We hold that the faith lives in the whole baptized people, not in the clergy alone. When councils define, it is the whole Body’s reception over time that seals the definition.

On Consulting the Faithful in Matters of Doctrine, by John Henry Newman. The record of the Arian century, when the body of the faithful preserved what many bishops lost. It proves the confession’s last clause from history, that the deposit lives in the whole baptized Body and not in the hierarchy alone, and the paper’s account of the faithful as receiver and witness rests on it.

The Bishop of Rome

We honor the Bishop of Rome as the first bishop of the Church, first in honor and first to be heard, and we hold that his office has the limits the first millennium knew, for his judgments awaited the reception of the whole Church. A real primacy, not a universal monarchy. The two readings below show both halves in the undivided Church’s own practice.

The Tome of St. Leo. Letter XXVIII, to Flavian, Bishop of Constantinople, read at the Council of Chalcedon. One event shows both halves of the clause. The fathers received the letter crying, Peter has spoken through Leo, and they received it after examining it against the faith of the Fathers, and the same council enacted a canon on Constantinople’s rank that Leo refused. Honor and limits together, in the undivided Church’s own practice.
Read online (HTML) · Download (PDF)

Letters on the Title Universal Bishop, by St. Gregory the Great. Six letters, written from the papal throne itself between 595 and 598, refusing the title universal bishop as a novelty that wrongs the whole episcopate. The confession’s limits on the primacy, stated by a pope, and one the whole Church calls a saint.

The one Church

We hold that the Church is one, because Christ has one Body. The divisions among Christians are wounds within her, not walls that cut her into several churches.

On the Unity of the Church, by St. Cyprian of Carthage, written in the year 251. The source of the confession’s hardest sentence, that outside the Church there is no salvation, from the father who also wrote that the episcopate is one and each bishop holds his part of it for the whole. Its fourth chapter comes down in two versions, one leaning on Peter and one on the equality of the apostles, and the editor’s footnote tells that story honestly.

Outside the Church there is no salvation

We hold, with the whole ancient Church, that outside the Church there is no salvation. But this old saying is easy to misread. Every communion has been tempted to read it as a sentence about itself, so that it means only Roman Catholics can be saved, or only the Orthodox. The confession rejects both readings, because both mistake a part of the Church for the whole of it.

The Church in this sentence is the whole Body of Christ. Her door is the one the Nicene Creed names, one Baptism for the forgiveness of sins, received in faith. Everyone who comes through that door is inside, whichever part of the divided Church he stands in. The divisions are real wounds, but a wound in a body does not make two bodies.

This is not a softer version of the saying. It still binds. There is no salvation apart from Christ, and the Church is His Body. What the confession refuses is the smaller claim that one communion’s borders are the Church’s borders. The churches themselves already half admit this at the font. Rome does not rebaptize Christians who come to her from other churches, and the East most often receives them by chrismation, which says the same thing in its own way. What the churches do with baptism is what they believe about the door.

None of this closes the door on the mercy of God. The Church has always allowed that God can save the person who desired baptism and never reached the font, and the person who never had a fair chance to hear. These are the old teachings called baptism of desire and invincible ignorance. And she has always permitted the hope, held as hope and not as a doctrine, that His mercy reaches further than we can see. The readings below show where that hope comes from, and how the Church has held it.

The Ascetical Homilies, by St. Isaac the Syrian, and On the Soul and the Resurrection, by St. Gregory of Nyssa. The Fathers of mercy that section 2 points to, the first-millennium root of the hope, held there as hope and not as dogma.

Dare We Hope “That All Men Be Saved”?, by Hans Urs von Balthasar, with the essay “Dare We Hope for the Salvation of All?”, by Kallistos Ware. The same hope stated in West and East, at the grade section 2 assigns it, held as hope, not asserted.

On indefectibility, and the preservation of the whole Church

We hold that Christ will preserve His whole Church to the end. The old word for this is indefectibility. Here too every communion has been tempted to claim the promise for itself alone, as though the see of Peter cannot fail, or as though Orthodoxy cannot fail. The confession says the promise was made to the whole Body, not to one part of it.

Held this way, the promise is modest. It does not mean a pope can never be wrong, or a council, or a whole region of the Church, for parts have gone wrong and history says so plainly. It means the faith itself can never be lost from the whole Church. If every church on earth had ever stopped confessing the Nicene Creed, the promise would have failed. That has never happened.

And you can watch the promise being kept in history. The Church has suffered tears and schisms from her earliest centuries, and every time, the faith came through on both sides of the tear. Churches that rejected a council ended up confessing the very faith that council defined. Sees fell, whole regions were lost, and the deposit of faith survived it all, carried by the whole even when a part stumbled. That is what it looks like when Christ keeps His promise.

Scripture, councils and popes: on authority

We hold that the bedrock of authority is not a court but the living God, who has made His will known in two ways. The first is the natural law, right and wrong written on the conscience, which any honest person can read. The second is His revelation, given in Scripture and carried by the Church. These are not rivals. The Bible is the supreme written witness of the faith, God-breathed, and clear in everything necessary for salvation, and the Church is the Body that received it, guards it, and reads it within her own life. Scripture, the Fathers, and the councils are not three competing courts. They are one deposit of faith, and the whole Church carries it.

So who settles a new question? Rome’s answer is a living office, the pope and his teaching authority, able to define new dogma and bind every conscience now. We honor the office and decline the claim, for one plain reason. The record shows that court defining things the whole Church never accepted, and reversing its own teaching in both directions, and a guarantee is only as good as its record. The East’s answer, that Orthodoxy alone is the Church and her councils bind, is declined for the same reason in the mirror. No one part can be the judge of the whole.

What do we have instead? For running the Church’s life today there is no lack. Scripture, the faith already received, the bishops in council, and Rome’s real primacy of initiative govern now, as they governed when St. Athanasius held the faith against a world gone Arian. What no one has in the moment is the guarantee that a brand-new definition belongs to the deposit. That certainty comes only as the whole Church receives it over time, the same way the seven councils were received. This is less than Rome promises, and we say so plainly. The believer caught in a live dispute is not left with nothing. He holds, as the faithful held before the councils vindicated them, to Scripture and the faith already received, trusting that Christ’s sheep know His voice.

Why 1054, and why later councils do not bind the whole

We hold that a council binds when the whole Church comes to confess the faith it defined. That is how the seven Ecumenical Councils came to bind all Christians. It was never the meeting alone that bound. It was the whole Body, East and West together, coming to confess what the council had defined, sometimes over generations. Even the churches that refused Ephesus and Chalcedon came, in time, to confess the faith those councils guarded, and that is what receiving a council means.

The Body had suffered tears before, but in 1054 East and West tore apart, the two great halves of Christendom from each other, and so much of the later tearing follows from it. The tear has never healed. Since then there has been no council of the whole Church, because the whole Church has never again sat together or confessed together. Rome has held councils since, up to Vatican I and Vatican II. The East has held her own, including the councils that defined the Palamite teaching. Every one of them was a council of one part, held while the other part stood apart and did not receive it.

So the confession treats those later definitions the way the first millennium would have. They are not condemned, for the whole never condemned them. They are not binding on all men, for the whole never received them. They stand as the teaching of the communion that made them, waiting on the reception of the whole Body. That is why this site keeps the seven councils and stops there. It is not doubt about God’s guidance. It is honesty about what the whole Church has actually received.

What it means to be merely catholic

We hold that catholic, with a small c, is older and bigger than Roman Catholic. The word comes from the Greek for according to the whole. The catholic faith is the faith of the whole Church, and the catholic Church is the whole Body of Christ. Roman Catholic names one communion inside that whole, the churches in communion with Rome, just as Orthodox names another. When this site says catholic, it means the whole.

We also hold that the Church is at once visible and invisible. She is visible, because Christ founded a real body you can find, with bishops, altars, fonts, and members, not an idea or a club of the sincere. And she is more than what you can see, because her borders are drawn by faith and baptism rather than by jurisdiction, and because God is not bound by His own sacraments. The visible and the invisible are not two churches. They are one Church, seen from earth and seen from heaven.

To be merely catholic, then, is to hold the faith the whole Church held before her great divisions, and to refuse to shrink it. The word merely is used in Lewis’s sense, not as a diminishment. It names the common core that all the apostolic communions still share, what was Christianity before it was Roman, Byzantine, or Reformed. A man can hold that core whole today, in whichever part of the divided Church he stands. To be merely catholic is to refuse to be less than the whole in order to remain loyal to a part.
